I. DAUBERT V. MERRELL DOW PHARMACEUTICALS, 509 U.S. 579 (1993)
================================================================================

CITATION: Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc., 509 U.S. 579 (1993)

COURT: United States Supreme Court

ISSUE:
What is the proper standard for admitting expert scientific testimony under
Federal Rule of Evidence 702?

FACTS:
Plaintiffs sued pharmaceutical company alleging birth defects caused by drug
Bendectin. Plaintiffs' expert witnesses offered testimony based on in vitro and
in vivo animal studies, chemical structure analysis, and reanalysis of
previously published epidemiological studies. Trial court granted summary
judgment to defendant, finding expert testimony inadmissible under Frye
"general acceptance" test.

HOLDING:
Federal Rule of Evidence 702 supersedes Frye standard in federal courts. Trial
judges must perform "gatekeeping" role to ensure expert testimony is both
relevant and reliable before admitting it.

FOUR-FACTOR RELIABILITY TEST (DAUBERT FACTORS):
[1] TESTABILITY: Can the theory or technique be tested? Has it been tested?
[2] PEER REVIEW: Has the technique been subjected to peer review and
    publication?
[3] ERROR RATE: What is the known or potential error rate? What standards and
    controls govern the technique?
[4] GENERAL ACCEPTANCE: Is the technique generally accepted in the relevant
    scientific community?

KEY LANGUAGE:
"The subject of an expert's testimony must be 'scientific knowledge.' The
adjective 'scientific' implies a grounding in the methods and procedures of
science. Similarly, the word 'knowledge' connotes more than subjective belief
or unsupported speculation."

"The trial judge must ensure that any and all scientific testimony or evidence
admitted is not only relevant, but reliable."

RELEVANCE TO CAD FORENSICS:
- Establishes testability requirement (DWG binary parsing must be reproducible)
- Requires peer review (cite NIST, ISO, SWGDE standards)
- Demands error rate quantification (CRC collision rates, hash verification
  accuracy)
- General acceptance in digital forensics community required

SOURCE: Cornell Law School, Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals
URL: https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/daubert_standard

II. FRYE V. UNITED STATES, 293 F. 1013 (D.C. CIR. 1923)
================================================================================

CITATION: Frye v. United States, 293 F. 1013 (D.C. Cir. 1923)

COURT: United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit

ISSUE:
Is expert testimony based on systolic blood pressure deception test (early
polygraph) admissible as scientific evidence?

FACTS:
Defendant charged with murder sought to introduce expert testimony regarding
results of systolic blood pressure deception test, an early form of lie
detector. Expert would testify that test showed defendant was truthful when
denying guilt.

HOLDING:
Expert testimony based on scientific technique is admissible only when the
technique is "generally accepted as reliable in the relevant scientific
community."

KEY LANGUAGE (FRYE TEST):
"Just when a scientific principle or discovery crosses the line between the
experimental and demonstrable stages is difficult to define. Somewhere in this
twilight zone the evidential force of the principle must be recognized, and
while courts will go a long way in admitting expert testimony deduced from a
well-recognized scientific principle or discovery, the thing from which the
deduction is made must be sufficiently established to have gained general
acceptance in the particular field in which it belongs."

CURRENT STATUS:
- Superseded in federal courts by Daubert (1993)
- Still followed in some state courts: California (modified), Illinois,
  Minnesota, New York, Pennsylvania, Washington

RELEVANCE TO CAD FORENSICS:
- In Frye jurisdictions, must demonstrate DWG forensics "generally accepted"
  in digital forensics community
- Cite NIST 800-86, ISO 27037, SWGDE standards as evidence of acceptance
- Emphasize binary file analysis, metadata extraction, hash verification are
  widely accepted techniques
- Novel techniques (LLM-enhanced reasoning) may struggle under Frye

SOURCE: Wikipedia, Frye Standard
URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frye_standard

SOURCE: NIJ, The Frye "General Acceptance" Standard
URL: https://nij.ojp.gov/nij-hosted-online-training-courses/law-101-legal-guide-forensic-expert/pretrial/pretrial-rules-evidence/frye-general-acceptance-standard

III. LORRAINE V. MARKEL AMERICAN INSURANCE CO., 241 F.R.D. 534 (D. MD. 2007)
================================================================================

CITATION: Lorraine v. Markel American Insurance Co., 241 F.R.D. 534 (D. Md.
          2007)

COURT: United States District Court for the District of Maryland

JUDGE: Magistrate Judge Paul W. Grimm (100-page opinion)

ISSUE:
How should courts authenticate electronically stored information (ESI) under
Federal Rules of Evidence 901-902?

FACTS:
Insurance coverage dispute involving extensive electronically stored
information. Court addressed authentication, hearsay, and best evidence rule
issues for digital evidence.

HOLDING:
Comprehensive framework for ESI authentication under FRE 901, with specific
guidance on hash values, metadata, and native format preservation.

KEY HOLDINGS:

1. AUTHENTICATION UNDER FRE 901
   - Hash values provide authentication through unique numerical identifiers
   - Metadata constitutes "distinctive characteristics" under Rule 901(b)(4)
   - Automated processes producing accurate results satisfy Rule 901(b)(9)

2. HASH VALUES
   "A hash value is a unique numerical identifier that can be assigned to a
   file, a group of files, or a portion of a file. Hash values can be inserted
   into original electronic documents when they are created to provide them
   with distinctive characteristics that will permit their authentication under
   Rule 901(b)(4)."

3. METADATA AS DISTINCTIVE CHARACTERISTIC
   "Because metadata shows the date, time, and identity of the creator of an
   electronic record, as well as all changes made to it, metadata is another
   distinctive characteristic of electronic evidence that can be used to
   authenticate under Rule 901(b)(4)."

4. NATIVE FORMAT PRESERVATION
   Under FRCP 34, parties may discover ESI in "native format" which includes
   metadata. Native format preservation strengthens authentication.

5. HEARSAY ANALYSIS
   Court addressed hearsay objections to computer-generated records,
   establishing framework for business records exception and non-hearsay
   characterization of automated data.

SIGNIFICANCE:
Described as "godfather of all cases" for authenticating electronic evidence.
First comprehensive judicial treatment of ESI authentication issues.

RELEVANCE TO CAD FORENSICS:
- SHA-256 hash values authenticate DWG files under Rule 901(b)(4)
- DWG metadata (TDCREATE, TDUPDATE, TDINDWG) are "distinctive characteristics"
- Forensic imaging process satisfies Rule 901(b)(9) reliable system
- Native .dwg format preservation (not PDF export) critical for authenticity

PRACTICAL APPLICATION:
1. Calculate hash values immediately upon receiving DWG files
2. Preserve metadata intact (do not strip or alter)
3. Maintain native format for analysis
4. Document forensic process as "reliable system"
5. Cite Lorraine in authentication brief as binding precedent

SOURCE: Wikipedia, Lorraine v. Markel American Insurance Co.
URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorraine_v._Markel_American_Insurance_Co.

SOURCE: EDDiscovery LLC, Historical ESI Highlights - Lorraine v. Markel
URL: https://www.ediscoveryllc.com/historical-esi-highlights-part-ix-lorraine-v-markel-am-ins-co/

IV. UNITED STATES V. COMPREHENSIVE DRUG TESTING, INC., 621 F.3D 1162 (2010)
================================================================================

CITATION: United States v. Comprehensive Drug Testing, Inc., 621 F.3d 1162
          (9th Cir. 2010) (en banc)

COURT: United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit (en banc)

ISSUE:
What Fourth Amendment protections apply when government seizes intermingled
electronic data during digital evidence searches?

FACTS:
Federal government obtained search warrant for drug testing records of 10
specific Major League Baseball players. During forensic acquisition, government
seized records of hundreds of other players not identified in warrant. Court
addressed whether seizure of intermingled digital data violated Fourth
Amendment.

HOLDING:
Government may seize "intermingled data for off-site review" but must follow
strict protocols to protect privacy and limit scope to warrant authorization.

KEY PRINCIPLES:

1. TAMURA DOCTRINE APPLICATION
   Court applied United States v. Tamura, 694 F.2d 591 (9th Cir. 1982): When
   documents are so intermingled that they cannot feasibly be sorted on-site,
   government may seize intermingled documents for off-site review, but must
   seal and hold pending magistrate approval.

2. PLAIN VIEW DOCTRINE WAIVER
   Court suggested magistrates should require government to waive reliance on
   plain view doctrine in digital searches, preventing "general rummaging"
   through electronic data.

3. SEGREGATION PROCEDURES
   "The process of segregating electronic data that is seizable from that which
   is not must not become a vehicle for the government to gain access to data
   which it has no probable cause to collect."

4. PROTOCOL REQUIREMENTS FOR DIGITAL SEARCHES
   - Clearly define scope of warrant
   - Use search protocols limiting examiner access
   - Implement technical safeguards (keyword searches, file type filters)
   - Provide for return of non-responsive data

SIGNIFICANCE:
Landmark Fourth Amendment precedent for digital evidence searches. Establishes
privacy protections when seizing electronic records.

RELEVANCE TO CAD FORENSICS:
- Forensic examiners must limit scope to relevant DWG files (not entire system)
- Use search protocols to avoid "general rummaging" through client data
- Keyword searches and file filtering demonstrate targeted approach
- Return non-responsive files to source party
- Privacy considerations when examining employee personal files on work systems

PRACTICAL APPLICATION:
1. Define narrow scope in forensic plan (DWG files related to Project X, not
   all CAD files)
2. Use file type filters (.dwg, .rvt) to limit acquisition
3. Implement keyword searches (project names, date ranges) to target relevant
   files
4. Document search protocol demonstrating targeted approach
5. Offer to return non-responsive files after analysis

CAVEAT:
Comprehensive Drug Testing is Ninth Circuit precedent and not binding in other
circuits. However, principles have been influential nationwide.

SOURCE: Casemine, U.S. v. Comprehensive Drug Testing, Inc.
URL: https://www.casemine.com/judgement/us/5914b00aadd7b04934750a2d

SOURCE: FindLaw, United States v. Comprehensive Drug Testing Inc.
URL: https://caselaw.findlaw.com/court/us-9th-circuit/1166919.html

V. NATIONAL UNION FIRE INS. CO. V. CUMMINS INC., 2025 WL 2532528 (2025)
================================================================================

CITATION: National Union Fire Ins. Co. of Pittsburgh, PA v. Cummins Inc., 2025
          WL 2532528 (S.D. Ind. Sept. 3, 2025)

COURT: United States District Court for the Southern District of Indiana

ISSUE:
Can a party bring counterclaim for intentional spoliation of digital evidence
in federal court?

FACTS:
Insurance coverage dispute involving alleged destruction of electronically
stored information. Defendant brought counterclaim alleging plaintiff
intentionally destroyed evidence, causing concrete litigation costs and
prejudice.

HOLDING:
Federal court applying Indiana law allowed intentional spoliation counterclaim
to proceed where party alleged concrete litigation costs and quantifiable
prejudice.

KEY PRINCIPLES:

1. INTENTIONAL SPOLIATION COUNTERCLAIM VIABLE
   Federal courts increasingly willing to recognize spoliation counterclaims
   when party demonstrates:
   - Intentional destruction (not negligence)
   - Concrete prejudice (not speculative harm)
   - Quantifiable litigation costs from evidence loss

2. CONCRETE HARM REQUIREMENT
   Party must allege specific, quantifiable harm (attorney's fees, expert costs,
   lost settlement opportunity) rather than generalized prejudice.

3. INTENTIONALITY STANDARD
   Higher threshold than FRCP 37(e) negligence standard. Must show deliberate
   destruction intended to deprive opponent of evidence's use in litigation.

SIGNIFICANCE:
2025 decision signals growing judicial willingness to impose consequences for
intentional digital evidence destruction. Demonstrates evolving spoliation
jurisprudence in electronic context.

RELEVANCE TO CAD FORENSICS:
- Intentional deletion of DWG files after litigation notice may support
  counterclaim
- Destruction of Revit journal files (.txt logs) documenting user actions could
  constitute intentional spoliation
- Reformatting hard drives or overwriting metadata after preservation duty
  arises creates liability risk
- Quantify concrete harm: cost of reconstructing timeline, expert fees for gap
  analysis, settlement value impact

PRACTICAL APPLICATION:
1. Issue litigation hold immediately upon dispute (document preservation duty)
2. Preserve all CAD files, backups, journal files, audit logs
3. Document preservation efforts for court submission
4. If opposing party destroys evidence, quantify concrete harm (expert costs,
   attorney's fees, lost settlement leverage)
5. Consider spoliation counterclaim if intentional destruction proven

SOURCE: Digital Warroom, What Is Spoliation Of Evidence?
URL: https://www.digitalwarroom.com/blog/what-is-digital-spoliation

VI. KUMHO TIRE CO. V. CARMICHAEL, 526 U.S. 137 (1999)
================================================================================

CITATION: Kumho Tire Co., Ltd. v. Carmichael, 526 U.S. 137 (1999)

COURT: United States Supreme Court

ISSUE:
Does Daubert reliability test apply to non-scientific expert testimony
(technical and specialized knowledge)?

FACTS:
Plaintiffs sued tire manufacturer after tire failure caused fatal accident.
Plaintiffs' expert (tire failure analyst) offered testimony based on visual and
tactile inspection. Trial court excluded testimony, finding expert's methodology
unreliable under Daubert.

HOLDING:
Daubert reliability test applies to ALL expert testimony (scientific, technical,
and specialized knowledge), not only scientific testimony. Trial judges have
discretion to determine which Daubert factors apply to specific expertise.

KEY PRINCIPLES:

1. DAUBERT APPLIES BEYOND SCIENCE
   "We conclude that Daubert's general holding - setting forth the trial judge's
   general 'gatekeeping' obligation - applies not only to testimony based on
   'scientific' knowledge, but also to testimony based on 'technical' and 'other
   specialized' knowledge."

2. FLEXIBLE APPLICATION
   Trial judge has "considerable leeway" in determining which Daubert factors
   are reasonable measures of reliability for particular testimony. Not all four
   factors must apply in every case.

3. EXPERIENCE-BASED TESTIMONY
   Expert testimony based primarily on experience (rather than academic research)
   is subject to Daubert scrutiny but may satisfy reliability through different
   factors.

SIGNIFICANCE:
Extended Daubert framework to technical and specialized knowledge, ensuring all
expert testimony undergoes reliability screening.

RELEVANCE TO CAD FORENSICS:
- Digital forensics is "technical" knowledge subject to Daubert (not purely
  scientific)
- Flexible application: Not all four Daubert factors equally applicable to
  digital forensics
- Experience-based expertise (10 years forensic casework) may satisfy
  reliability even without extensive peer-reviewed publications
- "Specialized knowledge" of DWG file formats falls under Kumho Tire scope

PRACTICAL APPLICATION:
1. Frame digital forensics as "technical and specialized knowledge" under FRE
   702
2. Emphasize flexible Daubert application (some factors more relevant than
   others)
3. If lacking peer-reviewed publications, emphasize practical experience and
   validated methodology
4. Combine academic credentials with hands-on casework experience

SOURCE: Cornell Law School, Kumho Tire Co. v. Carmichael
URL: https://www.law.cornell.edu/supct/html/97-1709.ZS.html

VII. STATE COURT PRECEDENTS - SPOLIATION
================================================================================

A. CALIFORNIA: CEDARS-SINAI MEDICAL CENTER V. SUPERIOR COURT (1998)

CITATION: Cedars-Sinai Medical Center v. Superior Court, 18 Cal. 4th 1 (1998)

COURT: California Supreme Court

ISSUE:
Does California recognize independent tort of intentional spoliation of evidence?

HOLDING:
No. California Supreme Court held that independent tort of intentional
spoliation is NOT cognizable against party-defendant in underlying case where
spoliation discovered before trial.

RATIONALE:
Adequate remedies exist within litigation (evidentiary sanctions, adverse
inference instructions, dismissal) without creating separate tort claim.

REMEDIES AVAILABLE:
- Adverse inference jury instructions (CACI No. 204)
- Monetary sanctions
- Evidentiary sanctions (exclude evidence)
- Case dismissal (extreme cases)

RELEVANCE TO CAD FORENSICS:
In California litigation, cannot bring separate spoliation tort claim against
opposing party who destroyed DWG files. Must seek remedies within existing case
through:
- FRCP 37(e) sanctions (if federal court)
- State procedural rules for sanctions
- Adverse inference instruction (CACI No. 204: "Willful Suppression of
  Evidence")

SOURCE: California Civil Jury Instructions (CACI) No. 204
URL: https://www.justia.com/trials-litigation/docs/caci/200/204/

B. ILLINOIS: BOYD V. TRAVELERS INSURANCE CO. (1998)

CITATION: Boyd v. Travelers Insurance Co., 166 Ill. 2d 188 (1995)

COURT: Illinois Supreme Court

ISSUE:
What duty exists to preserve evidence before litigation is filed?

HOLDING:
Duty to preserve relevant evidence arises when party knows or should know
litigation is probable, even before lawsuit filed.

KEY PRINCIPLE:
"Failure to produce relevant evidence because it was destroyed prior to filing
a lawsuit can be sanctioned because of the duty a potential litigant owes to
preserve relevant and material evidence."

REMEDIES:
- Dismissal under Illinois Supreme Court Rule 219(c)
- Negligent spoliation claim (separate cause of action)
- Adverse inference instructions
- Bar specific claims/defenses

RELEVANCE TO CAD FORENSICS:
In Illinois litigation involving DWG file destruction:
- Preservation duty arises when construction dispute emerges (before lawsuit)
- Demand letter, cease-and-desist, or arbitration notice triggers duty
- Deletion of CAD files after dispute arises = sanctionable conduct
- May pursue negligent spoliation claim as separate cause of action

SOURCE: Illinois State Bar Association, Sanctions and Spoliation
URL: https://www.isba.org/sections/civilpractice/newsletter/2011/08/sanctionsandspoliation

C. PENNSYLVANIA: SCHROEDER V. COMMONWEALTH (2013)

CITATION: Schroeder v. Commonwealth, Dep't of Transp., Bureau of Driver
          Licensing, 710 A.2d 23 (Pa. 1998)

COURT: Pennsylvania Commonwealth Court

ISSUE:
Does Pennsylvania recognize separate tort for negligent spoliation?

HOLDING:
No. Pennsylvania refuses to recognize separate claim for "negligence" for
careless destruction of evidence.

DUTY TO PRESERVE:
Pennsylvania law imposes duty on all parties to civil dispute to preserve
evidence within their care, custody, or control.

SANCTIONS FACTORS:
Court considers:
- Degree of fault of party who destroyed evidence
- Degree of prejudice suffered by opposing party
- Availability of lesser sanction protecting opposing party's rights

RELEVANCE TO CAD FORENSICS:
In Pennsylvania litigation:
- No separate negligent spoliation tort (unlike Illinois)
- Sanctions available within existing case
- Court weighs fault, prejudice, and lesser sanctions
- Intentional destruction receives harsher sanctions than negligence

SOURCE: Pittsburgh Litigation Lawyer, Spoliation in PA
URL: https://pittsburgh-litigation-lawyer.com/duty-to-preserve-evidence-spoliation-in-pa/

VIII. FRE 702 POST-2023 AMENDMENT CASE LAW
================================================================================

A. 2023 AMENDMENT OVERVIEW

Effective December 1, 2023, FRE 702 amended to address widespread misapplication
by courts.

KEY CHANGES:
1. Proponent must show "it is more likely than not" that requirements met
   (preponderance standard)
2. Expert's opinion must not extend beyond what methodology can reliably support
3. Courts must evaluate methodology application, not just methodology in general

ADVISORY COMMITTEE NOTE:
"Many courts have held that the critical questions of the sufficiency of an
expert's basis, and the application of the expert's methodology, are questions
of weight and not admissibility. These rulings are an incorrect application of
Rules 702 and 104(a)."

B. IMPLICATIONS FOR DIGITAL FORENSICS

POST-2023 HEIGHTENED SCRUTINY:
- Proponent bears burden to demonstrate reliability by preponderance (more
  likely than not)
- Cannot rely on expert's credentials alone
- Must show methodology properly applied to case facts
- Conclusions must be supported by sufficient data

ANTICIPATED DEFENSE CHALLENGES:
- "Expert's conclusions extend beyond what binary parsing can reliably show"
- "Expert did not properly apply methodology to incomplete DWG file set"
- "Expert's data set insufficient to support sweeping conclusions about
  intentional tampering"

DEFENSIVE STRATEGIES:
- Explicitly address sufficiency of data in expert report
- Limit conclusions to what methodology supports ("CRC mismatch indicates file
  modification" vs. "Defendant committed fraud")
- Document methodology application step-by-step
- Demonstrate preponderance standard satisfied ("more likely than not that
  methodology properly applied")

SOURCE: Harvard Law Review, Federal Rule of Evidence 702
URL: https://harvardlawreview.org/print/vol-138/federal-rule-of-evidence-702/

SOURCE: FedSoc Blog, 2023 Amendments to Federal Rules of Evidence
URL: https://fedsoc.org/commentary/fedsoc-blog/a-brief-guide-to-the-2023-amendments-to-the-federal-rules-of-evidence-1

IX. PRACTICAL LITIGATION STRATEGY BASED ON CASE LAW
================================================================================

A. AUTHENTICATION STRATEGY (LORRAINE V. MARKEL)

PROCEDURE:
[1] Calculate SHA-256 hash values immediately upon receiving DWG files
[2] Document hash values in forensic workbook with date/time/examiner
[3] Preserve native format (.dwg, .rvt) with metadata intact
[4] Use forensic imaging software to create bit-for-bit copies
[5] Re-calculate hash values before analysis to verify integrity
[6] Cite Lorraine v. Markel in authentication brief

AUTHENTICATION BRIEF ARGUMENT:
"Under Lorraine v. Markel American Insurance Co., 241 F.R.D. 534 (D. Md. 2007),
hash values provide authentication through unique numerical identifiers
satisfying FRE 901(b)(4). The Court in Lorraine held that metadata constitutes
a 'distinctive characteristic' under Rule 901(b)(4). Here, SHA-256 hash values
and DWG metadata (TDCREATE, TDUPDATE, TDINDWG) authenticate the files as
genuine copies of the files produced by Defendant."

B. DAUBERT DEFENSE STRATEGY (DAUBERT, KUMHO TIRE)

DAUBERT BRIEF STRUCTURE:
I. EXPERT QUALIFICATIONS (demonstrate knowledge, skill, experience)
II. METHODOLOGY RELIABILITY
    A. Testability - DWG binary parsing reproducible
    B. Peer Review - NIST 800-86, ISO 27037, SWGDE standards
    C. Error Rate - CRC32 collision probability quantified
    D. General Acceptance - Digital forensics community consensus
III. FLEXIBLE APPLICATION (Kumho Tire discretion)
    - Not all factors equally applicable to technical knowledge
    - Emphasis on methodology validation and practical experience
IV. POST-2023 FRE 702 COMPLIANCE
    - Preponderance standard satisfied
    - Conclusions limited to methodology support
    - Proper application to case facts demonstrated

C. SPOLIATION CLAIMS (NATIONAL UNION FIRE INS. CO., STATE LAW)

SPOLIATION COUNTERCLAIM ELEMENTS (WHERE VIABLE):
[1] Pending or probable litigation
[2] Defendant's knowledge of litigation
[3] Intentional destruction of relevant evidence
[4] Concrete prejudice (quantifiable harm)
[5] Damages proximately caused by spoliation

QUANTIFY CONCRETE HARM:
- Expert witness fees to reconstruct timeline: $X
- Attorney's fees for additional discovery: $Y
- Lost settlement leverage (reduced settlement value): $Z
- Costs of forensic gap analysis: $A
TOTAL QUANTIFIABLE DAMAGES: $[X+Y+Z+A]

D. FRYE JURISDICTIONS (CALIFORNIA, ILLINOIS, NEW YORK, PENNSYLVANIA)

GENERAL ACCEPTANCE DEMONSTRATION:
[1] NIST SP 800-86 (government endorsement)
[2] ISO/IEC 27037:2012 (international standard)
[3] SWGDE Best Practices (industry consensus)
[4] IACIS, HTCIA, SANS training curricula (professional acceptance)
[5] EnCase, FTK, X-Ways software adoption (widespread use)
[6] Expert declarations from other digital forensics professionals

BRIEF ARGUMENT:
"Binary file format analysis is generally accepted in the digital forensics
community, as evidenced by NIST SP 800-86, ISO/IEC 27037:2012, and SWGDE Best
Practices. Forensic software tools (EnCase, FTK) incorporate file format parsing
capabilities, demonstrating widespread acceptance. Professional organizations
(IACIS, HTCIA) teach binary file analysis in certification programs. Under Frye
v. United States, 293 F. 1013 (D.C. Cir. 1923), this methodology has crossed
from 'experimental' to 'demonstrable' stage and gained general acceptance."

CONCLUSION
================================================================================

Case law establishes robust framework for admitting DWG/Revit forensic evidence
in federal and state courts:

DAUBERT FRAMEWORK (Federal courts + majority states):
- Four-factor reliability test applies to digital forensics
- Flexible application under Kumho Tire for technical knowledge
- Post-2023 heightened scrutiny of methodology application

FRYE FRAMEWORK (CA, IL, NY, PA, WA):
- "General acceptance" test satisfied by NIST/ISO/SWGDE standards
- Emphasis on community consensus over individual validation

AUTHENTICATION (Lorraine v. Markel):
- Hash values authenticate digital files
- Metadata provides distinctive characteristics
- Native format preservation critical

SPOLIATION (National Union Fire Ins. Co., state law):
- Intentional destruction creates liability risk
- Concrete harm quantification required for counterclaim
- Preservation duty arises when litigation foreseeable

Successful litigation requires citing these precedents in authentication briefs,
Daubert/Frye responses, and spoliation motions to establish legal foundation
for DWG/Revit forensic evidence admissibility.
